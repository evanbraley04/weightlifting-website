Why Dieting is Crucial to Weightlifting

Dieting is crucial to making progress in the gym, but not in the way you might expect. When you lift, there are essentially three main things you can do with your weight. You can cut to lose weight, maintain your current weight, or bulk to gain weight. If you are trying to lose fat and slim out, a cut might be the best thing for you to do. If you are trying to maintain your current physique and maybe get a little more defined, then maintenance is probably the way to go. If you are trying to build a large amount of muscle mass in a relatively short period of time, a bulk is the best option. These three options essentially rely on the amount of calories that you intake daily. Your body has something known as a TDEE, or total daily energy expenditure, which essentially equates to how many calories your body burns in a day. Thus, depending on what your goals are, you need to eat an amount relative to your TDEE in order to fluctuate your weight. This is the most important component of dieting for bodybuilding. In addition to this, you want your protein intake to be pretty high, about one gram per lb that you weigh. Protein plays a large part in building muscle so you want to make sure you are eating enough of it. In regards to eating healthy, or eating certain foods, that isn't really a large component of dieting in concern to bodybuilding. Obviously eating healthy foods is better, and will lead you to having a healthier body overall, but there isn't a magical formula or certain foods that you can or can't eat. Overall, dieting, when it comes to bodybuilding, is all about fluctuating your weight in order to meet your various goals, while also ensuring a good intake of protein in order to ensure muscle building.

My experience with dieting

I've made some mistakes in the past with dieting. When I first started lifting I had no idea what I was doing and just kind of ate. This was detrimental to making progress and it wasn't until I started paying closer attention to dieting that I started to make noticeable progress. For me personally, I started in a place where I was very underweight, so my initial goal was to put on lots of mass. I did this by going on a "bulk". Essentially, I just ate significantly more than before. I found a website that helped calculate my TDEE, which for me I found to be roughly 2,800 calories per day when accounting for the amount of physical activity I was doing everyday. I started by first adjusting to eating at my maintenance. Before I was eating significantly under my maintenance, which was probably a large contributor to me being underweight. Once I got used to that, I slowly upped my intake overtime, to the point where roughly a year later I was eating 4,000 calories per day. One of the most important tools that I have found for staying on track with my diet has been a dedicated food tracker app. I personally used FoodNoms, which can be found on the appstore. An app like this helps ensure that you are meeting your goals everyday, which is important because it is extremely easy to overestimate or underestimate how much you are really eating. For me personally, I am still currently bulking as I haven't reached a point yet where I feel I need to start losing weight. My current plan is to continue my bulk until I reach roughly 160 lbs and then reassess from there. Throughout my journey with dieting I've come to the realization that although dieting isn't really complex per se, it is entirely individual and personal. What works for some people, may not work for others, so it is important to experiment and grow to figure out what helps you personally achieve the progress that suits your goals.
